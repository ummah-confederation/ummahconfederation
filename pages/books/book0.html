BOOK 0 : ON TRUTH

PREAMBLE

Below inshaallah will be explained regarding truth, and our treatment of truth.

TITLE 1 : TRUTH

Article 1 – Meaning of Truth

Truth is generally defined as the property of statements, beliefs, or propositions that accurately accord with reality.

TITLE 2 : THE IDEA OF DHIKR

Article 1 – Meaning of Dhikr

Dhikr is the act of re-remembering the truth. Could be done in your heart (affirming truth with your heart), and mouth (either for your own re-affirmation / re-alignation, or for the purpose of truth conveyance to others).

Article 2 – Acts of Dhikr

  1. Reading: The act of discovering truth either via text or directly to nature.

  2. Remembrance (Dhikr): The act of affirming truth with your heart or sirr mouth.

  3. Recitation (Iqra): The act of reciting truth via jahr mouth.

  4. Da'wah: The act of inviting people towards truth in an inviting way.

TITLE 3 : VARIOUS PRINCIPLES OF DHIKR

Article 1 – Dhikr Is Not the Creation of Truth

Dhikr is the affirmation of truth, not the creation of truth. To think of dhikr as creating truth would be like praising God as if He were not already praiseworthy. Dhikr is not meant to sugarcoat reality. Dhikr is not for flattery or "ass-kissing," like praising a boss just to satisfy their ego or cover their insecurity. Dhikr is simply the affirmation of what is true. God is Z even if you never say that God is Z. Z does not wait for your affirmation in order to be Z. Z is self-sufficient, with or without your affirmation.

Article 2 – False or Partial Truth Produces False Response

When what is believed to be true is in fact false, incomplete, or selectively chosen, the response that follows will necessarily be misaligned with reality. A person does not react to reality as it is, but to the version of truth they have accepted. If that accepted “truth” is partial, emotionally convenient, or born of desire rather than reality, then judgment, emotion, and action will be directed toward something that does not fully exist. Thus, correct dhikr requires not merely remembrance, but remembrance grounded in complete and accurate truth. Where truth is truncated, reaction is misdirected.

Article 3 – Dhikr Aligns You to Truth, Not Vice Versa

You are no longer saying, “God, please be this,” but rather, “I acknowledge that God is this.” This is a fundamental shift: you do not define or dictate what God SHOULD be for you; instead, you define how you SHOULD be toward God. When you stop trying to bend reality to your preferences and instead align yourself with reality as it is, your reaction to reality becomes clearer, free from emotional distortion. You are no longer attempting to control reality; you are controlling yourself and your mode of encountering reality so that you can adapt to it (bi idhnillah). This alignment allows you to respond appropriately to what is actually there.

Article 3 – Dhikr is Not a Transaction

Dhikr is not a transaction of the form "I praise, You reward." There is nothing to negotiate because dhikr is not a bargaining tool. That's why when you want or need something from God, you ask Him through direct prayer, not through dhikr with hidden motives. Dhikr has a different function: it aligns you with reality as it is, allowing you to perceive it without emotional distortion and to respond to it well in order to achieve what you need. Prayer is asking; dhikr is internal realignment.

Article 4 – Dhikr as a Resting Place

If truth does not depend on you, then you are freed from an impossible burden. You do not have to hold the universe together. You do not have to feel that you have earned your existence through your own power. Of course, you must seek God's approval—that is essential—but you do not secure it by feeling that your performance has contributed something that makes God more worthy or more complete. God's worthiness does not depend on your contributions. That realization is deeply calming and humbling. Dhikr becomes a resting place and a counseling place—where you are constantly reminded of truth and humbled by it (coz either sometimes we are wrong or unable to apply truth fully via our strength but rather God's strength, or the truth content itself is humbling us ), constantly reshaped internally to be molded by it—rather than a stage where you are inflated by the feeling that your performance contributes to God’s worthiness.

Article 5 – "I Am as My Servant Thinks of Me"

Allah says: "I am as My servant thinks of Me." {Hadith Qudsi, Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim}. This does not mean you create God by believing in Him, or that truth bends to your psychology. Rather, it means this: your experience of God corresponds to your assumption about God. God is what He is—but you encounter Him through the lens you carry. If you assume God is angry, you do not make God angry; you place yourself in a posture where anger is what you encounter. If you assume God is merciful, you do not bend God toward mercy; you become receptive to the mercy already present. However, your behavior must also align with this understanding. You cannot continuously claim that God is merciful while persistently doing things that provoke His anger. Yes, God is Merciful, but God is also Just. You cannot deliberately conceal one truth while invoking another through dhikr. You cannot adopt a partial truth. The lens you carry must be informed by full truth, not partial truth born of wishful thinking. That is why the real question is this: does dhikr (the re-remembering of truth) actually change you, or are you using it as a form of self-deception?

Article 6 – Dhikr is an Act of Ibadah

Even though dhikr is only meant to align you with the truth (it does not benefit God in any way), it is still an act of ibadah that God rewards. This is part of God’s mercy. So be grateful, and be khusyu, and make dhikr an act of worship, rather than a way of making yourself feel like the most aligned-to-the-truth person of all.

Article 7 – The Prayer for a Good End

Blessed is the one who dies in a good state, approaching God from the angle of mercy at the time of death. This is not guaranteed by the state of your heart at that final moment alone—you cannot control your own feelings—but by the true mercy of God, which moves you to remember Him at the time of death. Therefore, continually ask God for guidance: that He grants you constant dhikr and the grace to meet Him well, acknowledging His mercy even as you acknowledge your need for it.

Article 8 – Truth Is Always at Stake

Every dhikr we say is tied to a real awareness of what is at stake—what depends on Allah, what could be lost without Him, what mistakes or misunderstandings the phrase corrects, or what ultimate truth it points to. Dhikr should not be done mechanically, but with knowledge of why you are doing that specific dhikr. You must know what is at stake when you are saying it. Without this awareness, dhikr becomes shallow—why do something if there is no stake? Are you just performing empty motions? The structure is: Dhikr Phrase → Recognition of What is at Stake → Conscious Saying. Example Applications:

Dhikr Phrase Stake Awareness Examples
Maha Suci Allah (Subhanallah) The risk of thinking God is imperfect or unjust when human-made injustices occur. Such thoughts can harm your relationship with destiny and, in extreme cases, affect mental health. "Subhanallah amma yashifun" reminds us that God is pure and beyond human faults; injustices in the world are not God's doing.
Segala Puji bagi Allah (Alhamdulillah) The danger of praising or relying on anything other than Allah, which can lead to unproductive disappointment or unhappiness. "Alhamdulillahirabbil'alamin" reminds us that Allah is the true Rabb, not your wealth or fame. When these are gone, you understand that praise and expectations belong to the Rabb who always exist till this time to sustain you.
Allah Maha Besar (Allahu Akbar) The fear, stress, or intimidation caused by worldly power, status, or events that seem overwhelming, which can make you feel defeated before the battle even begins. Like a soldier facing a great army, "Allahuakbar" reminds us that God is greater, giving courage and perspective

TITLE 4 : OUTPUT OF DHIKR

Dhikr usually has a transformative effect—not magically, but by aligning all your emotions and thoughts with the truth. This alignment is, biidhnillah, what strengthens you in difficult situations. But remember, dhikr is not the only option. When the stakes are very high, deadly, beyond your control, you can turn to direct prayer. Transformation begins within the self, and as a result, outward change often follows naturally or can be intentionally encouraged.
